About Me

I am a PhD Candidate in English Literature at the University of Notre Dame, specializing in computational literary analysis, cognitive narratology and the nineteenth-century novel. My first monograph-in-progress, Why Characters Seem Real offers the first theory of literary character realism that takes into account the social psychology of the reader. My research has been supported by the Notebaert Premier Fellowship, the Notre Dame Institute for Advanced Study Distinguished Graduate Fellowship, the Nanovic Institute Dissertation Completion Fellowship, and the International Society for the Study of Narrative's Alan Nadel Prize. My early work on aesthetic theory and social mobility in the Victorian novel has been published by Journal of Victorian Culture

I am dedicated to fostering interdisciplinary dialogue, collaboration and community in the academy. To this end, in 2020 I founded Cyborg, a guest speaker and seminar series at the University of Notre Dame focused on interdisciplinary research approaches that cross STEM/humanities boundaries. I am also currently working on a collaborative projects using quantitative methods to analyze written text with information scientists at the Navari Center for Digital Scholarship, and computer scientists at the Lucy Institute for Data and Society

I am particularly interested in developing new digital methods for studying charactierization in written fiction, and I am delighted to hear from potential collaborators on this topic.
